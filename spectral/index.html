A current project of interest involves developing provably optimal solution guarantees to nonconvex optimization problems under certain regimes. An example problem instance is the well-known \(\mathbb{Z}/2\) quadratic program: \[ \max_{x \in \{-1,1\}^n} x^T A x, \] where \(A \succeq 0\) is typically assumed positive semidefinite. With no domain restriction, the optimal \(x\) is the leading eigenvector of \(A\). By restricting to the Boolean cube, this becomes NP-hard (though it can be approximated using semidefinite programming relaxations and the sum-of-squares/Lasserre hierarchy; see eg. ). This problem (sometimes called the little Grothendieck problem ) arises in various important settings, eg. on a graph , stochastic block models/community detection in network analysis , and group synchonization problems (over \(\mathbb{Z}/2\)).

In \(\mathbb{Z}/2\) synchronization problems, we try to recover an unknown (Bernoulli) signal \(x \in \{-1,1\}^n\) given an observed matrix \(A = xx^T + \sigma W\), where \(\sigma >0\) is some noise level and \(W = W^T\) is a symmetric random noise matrix (eg. a Wigner matrix with Gaussian entries). Then \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) arises naturally in the sense that its solution is the maximum likelihood (or maximum a posteriori, in the Bayesian regime assuming a uniform prior on \(x\)) estimator for \(x\), i.e. the solution to \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) maximizes the probability of recovering \(x\) given our observation. % we have a distribution over matrices A observed given the unknown parameter x

This also arises from looking at certain low-rank or spiked Bernoulli models in the context of cancer diagnosis. In particular, in the case of a random process generating Bernoulli observations, the matrix \(A\) arises as a sample covariance matrix. Again, \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) has a clear interpretation: we are performing a kind of PCA, but want our estimators to come from the observation space, the Boolean hypercube.

The general difficulty in solving \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) is that the discrete nature of the hypercube results in a (nonconvex) optimization landscape plagued with ``spurious” local optima. However, under certain conditions the problem is empirically well-behaved. Recently, have shown that the natural estimator \(\hat{x} = \text{sign}(v)\), where \(v\) is the leading eigenvector of \(A\), exactly recovers \(x\) (in the sense that \(\hat{x} = \pm x\) with probability approaching \(1\) as \(n \to \infty\)), provided that the noise \(\sigma\) is beneath a threshold. This suggests that under the specified regime, we may have an exact solution to \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\), albeit with high probability.

A natural question is to what extent this can translate into exact results for solving \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\). In other words, can we assume certain conditions on \(A\) to make \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) tractable (even solvable in closed form)? A reasonable conjecture along these lines is that if the observed \(A\) has leading eigenvalue sufficiently well-separated from the rest of the spectrum, the above estimator \(\hat{x}\) solves \(\eqref{eqn:xtax}\) exactly with no spurious optima. Note that this is likely related to the famous BBP phase transition in random matrix theory, which describes when a small perturbation of a random noise (covariance) matrix is detectable based on outliers in its spectrum. This kind of assumption is rather general and is naturally satisfied in many applications; in particular, whenever it is appropriate to use low-rank or spiked/planted models for \(A\), we expect this to occur.

This project fits into a broader recent theme (of general interest) that nonconvex problems are often quite benign under certain circumstances, which helps explain why simple methods and heuristics have often performed well in practice (see for a recent survey along these lines).
